

# @GitHub I supose that the design process policy is a good place to go next?

Yes—**Design Practice Policy** is the right next policy. It is the missing bridge between research (what we know or suspect), decision practice (what is authorized), and stories (what can be built).

The existing design corpus has many useful, valid design artifacts—identity, CLI/REST, continuity, cache, interface, verification, and phase plans—but it uses varied structures and does not yet consistently distinguish a proposal, an unresolved question, a binding constraint, a decision requiring ADR, and an implementation-ready slice.

## What design policy must do

Create:

```text
docs/process/design.md
```

It should establish that a design is **a bounded proposed shape**, not a decision, implementation, or guaranteed plan.

```clojure
(δ design-brief
   research-inputs
   constraints
   alternatives
   open-questions
   -> proposed-shape)
```

Here, `δ` should represent the design transformation: it composes known evidence, constraints, and possible approaches into a testable proposed shape. Like η, μ, Π, and λ, it remains valid design data before a tool can execute every part of it.

## Core distinctions

The policy needs to enforce these separations:


| Artifact/claim | What it does | What it cannot do alone |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Research finding | Bounds what evidence supports | Choose an architecture |
| Design | Proposes system shape, constraints, interfaces, and tradeoffs | Authorize a consequential choice |
| ADR / decision | Explicitly chooses among alternatives | Implement or verify the choice |
| Story | Commits a bounded implementation slice | Define architecture from scratch |
| Verification record | Shows what a procedure observed | Decide that results are sufficient |
| Review/acceptance record | Records authorized disposition | Rewrite the original evidence |

That distinction matches what the existing design documents already want to do. For example, `verification-architecture.md` declares schema ownership, validation boundaries, adapter laws, CI evidence, and implementation slices—which are design constraints and inputs to future decisions/work, not all automatically accepted policy.

## Required design contract

A material design should contain:

- **Purpose and decision/use context** — what question it resolves or what it enables.
- **Scope and non-goals** — including the excluded future work.
- **Inputs and authority** — relevant research, ADRs, source facts, product constraints, and existing implementation facts.
- **Problem model / constraints** — what must remain true, including epistemic and identity constraints.
- **Proposed shape** — components, data/contracts, boundary placement, interactions, state/failure behavior, and lifecycle where applicable.
- **Alternatives and tradeoffs** — including why not to choose them now.
- **Open questions** — each marked as research, design, decision, dependency, or implementation uncertainty.
- **Decision candidates** — items that must become ADRs before execution can responsibly depend on them.
- **Verification strategy** — what would demonstrate the design’s relevant properties.
- **Implementation implications** — candidate slices, dependency order, and migration/compatibility needs.

Designs should link inputs via typed relations: `informs`, `sources`, `requires`, `implements`, and later `supersedes`, not just prose hyperlinks.

## Readiness boundary

The policy should state a precise rule:

> A story may depend on a design that retains open questions only when those questions cannot materially change the story’s declared scope, contracts, verification, or safety/reversibility profile.

Otherwise the design is not a failure; it is **still in the correct state: unresolved**. Its next output is research, an ADR, a spike, a narrower design, or a revised plan—not implementation theater.

## Links to engineering kernel

This policy should explicitly use the engineering form:

```clojure
(η Discovery
   (-> (-> Describe specify define)
       (-> μ shape extern domain infra))
   Π)
```

Design operates in the middle:

```text
Research / η observation
  -> Describe
  -> specify
  -> Design proposed shape
  -> define laws/contracts
  -> μ construction
```

It cannot skip `define`. A design that proposes a persisted artifact, port, or foreign boundary needs to name the future `law.*`, `shape.*`, `extern.*`, `domain.*`, and `infra.*` obligations—or explicitly explain why a layer is not relevant.

## First dogfood corpus

Use three kinds of existing documents as examples, not immediate rewrite targets:

- `docs/designs/artifact-identity-model.md` and `continuity-gradient-model.md`: design proposals that informed ADR 001.
- `docs/designs/verification-architecture.md`: a cross-cutting engineering design which should identify its proposed laws, adapter contracts, and assurance evidence.
- `docs/designs/phase-1-narrowed-decisions.md`: a decision inventory/position document that needs explicit separation between recommendations and actual ADR acceptance.

Then write a good/bad design template and use it before revising the assurance-story batch.
