You are reaching for a real thing: not rigid bureaucracy, and not vague principles either—a system that lets agents operate concretely while preserving the fact that understanding is partial, revisable, and socially accepted.

The drafts are not failures. They are **discovery artifacts**: they record the unresolved design problem that the process must model. In particular, the core insight is already present: each phase should leave another actor with enough inspectable evidence to continue responsibly.

## Artifact is correct

Yes, “artifact” is the right umbrella term for these text files—but **artifact alone does not mean evidence**.

An artifact is any durable, addressable thing used or produced by work:

```text
Markdown document
EDN record
Kanban card
Git commit
Test output
CI report
Benchmark dataset
Search result set
Agent ledger entry
Generated graph projection
```

Whether an artifact is evidence depends on its relation to a claim:


| Status | Meaning |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Artifact** | A durable object exists |
| **Observation** | It directly records something encountered or executed |
| **Evidence** | It is relevant to assessing a particular claim |
| **Finding** | An interpretation derived from one or more pieces of evidence |
| **Proposal** | A suggested action or explanation |
| **Decision** | An explicitly accepted commitment |
| **Verification record** | Evidence that a declared check was actually run |

For example, your meta-workflow document is an artifact. Its descriptions of the problem you are experiencing are first-person observations. Its proposed phase model is a provisional design proposal. It becomes evidence for a process decision only when the decision record explicitly cites it and explains how it supports the chosen rule.

That distinction matters because agents otherwise treat “a Markdown file exists” as “the file proves its own assertions.”

## The hard universal rule

I do think there is one universal constraint underneath what you are trying to say:

> **No actor may present an inference, plan, completion, or decision as more certain or more accepted than the evidence and explicit acceptance behind it.**

That is not “every task requires mutation testing.” It is not “every change requires a research report.” It is a truthfulness and provenance rule.

It produces concrete rules for agents:

- They may say, “I observed X.”
- They may say, “Given X and Y, I infer Z,” while preserving X and Y.
- They may say, “I propose Z,” without treating Z as accepted.
- They may say, “Tests passed,” only with the actual command/run evidence.
- They may not say, “This is complete/correct/approved,” unless the appropriate reviewer or acceptance process establishes that claim.
- They may stop and request clarification when no adequate evidence path exists.

That supports flexibility because the required *kind and amount* of evidence is proportional to the consequence of the claim.

## Principle plus policy

The resolution is a two-level system—not principles alone, and not universal detailed rules.

```text
Constitution: invariant limits on claims and authority
  -> stable, short, universal

Policies: concrete rules for a work context
  -> versioned, changeable, machine-checkable

Plans/cards: local commitments under those policies
  -> scoped, reviewable, revisable

Evidence records: observations of what happened
  -> append-only where possible
```

The constitution should state **what cannot be silently collapsed**:

- Observation is not inference.
- Inference is not decision.
- Decision is not implementation.
- Passing a check is not proof of all desired behavior.
- A plan is not evidence of completion.
- Similarity is not identity.
- Absence of accessible evidence is not evidence of absence.
- A process exception is not a hidden rule change.

Policies then decide whether a particular change needs mutation testing, property tests, review, a research brief, or only formatting verification.

## Agents need selectable policies

Your experience with mutation testing is instructive. Humans can exercise context-sensitive judgment cheaply; an agent will tend to optimize whatever it sees, including a single test suite. The policy should therefore be selected by **risk profile**, not applied universally.

```clojure
{:work/profile :routine-code-change
 :required [:task :tests :review-evidence]
 :recommended [:lint :targeted-regression]
 :not-required [:research-report :mutation-test]}

{:work/profile :boundary-or-contract-change
 :required [:design-link :contract-tests :negative-tests
            :state-preservation-test :review-evidence]
 :recommended [:property-tests :targeted-mutation]
 :not-required [:broad-literature-research]}

{:work/profile :architectural-decision
 :required [:research-brief :alternatives :adr :explicit-acceptance]
 :recommended [:spike :independent-review]
 :not-required [:full-implementation]}

{:work/profile :epistemic-claim-change
 :required [:provenance :tier-label :counterevidence-search
            :human-acceptance-before-promotion]
 :recommended [:reproducible-evaluation]}
```

An agent first identifies or asks for the profile; the policy gives it explicit commands and deliverables. The profile itself is a **provisional classification** until a human or approved gate accepts it for material work.

## Your phases are useful—but separate axes

The drafts currently conflate three different things:


| Concept | What it describes | Recommended form |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Modes** | Repeated cognitive moves | Explore, orient, commit, act, verify, reflect |
| **Artifact lifecycle** | Maturity of the work/claim | Intake, research, design, decision, plan, task, implementation, accepted result |
| **Agent architecture** | Who can observe, act, or delegate | Muse, phase agent, ledger, mailbox, tools |

All three are legitimate. They should not be one state machine.

### Modes

Your `explore -> orient -> commit -> act -> verify -> reflect` model is strong because it can occur at **any** lifecycle stage.

A developer might explore before implementing. A reviewer explores before verifying. A research agent orients after source discovery. A planner reflects after a task proves too large.

The missing piece is that “commit” should create a bounded, reviewable artifact:

```clojure
{:commitment/id ...
 :actor/id ...
 :target/artifact ...
 :intent "Determine whether the existing Mongo adapter enforces all registered write schemas."
 :basis [observation-ids...]
 :scope {:in [...] :out [...]}
 :stop-conditions [...]
 :status :active}
```

It is not a promise of success. It is an explicit statement of current intent and its evidence basis.

### Artifact lifecycle

This can remain simple:

```text
intake
  -> inquiry
  -> research / exploration
  -> proposal
  -> design
  -> decision, if architectural policy is needed
  -> plan
  -> executable task
  -> implementation evidence
  -> review
  -> accepted outcome
```

Not every request traverses every stage. A typo correction can go `intake -> task -> review -> accepted outcome`. A new identity model can travel through all stages.

### Agent architecture

Muses, phase agents, isolated ledgers, mailboxes, and the actor model are not needed to rewrite PROCESS.md. They are a possible future architecture for **how agents operate under the process**.

Your own note—“do not bring that over here”—is exactly right. Preserve it as an open design hypothesis, but do not let it leak into present governance. If and when the project needs autonomous multi-agent orchestration, create a separate research brief/design/ADR chain.

## Do not turn the graph into a gate yet

The Bayesian / graph-confidence idea is promising, but premature as an operational requirement.

A graph can later assist discovery:

```text
user request
  -> candidate artifact anchors
  -> supporting/contradicting relations
  -> confidence-ranked next reading or question
```

But it must not decide that an artifact “supports” a hypothesis without recording:

- Who/what asserted the relationship.
- What relation type is claimed.
- What exact evidence supports it.
- Whether it is observed, derived, provisional, or accepted.
- Confidence model/version and its limitations.
- A human path to challenge or reject it.

For now, use a boring, explicit relation record:

```clojure
{:relation/id ...
 :from/artifact-id ...
 :relation/type :supports
 :to/claim-id ...
 :assertion-tier :provisional
 :asserted-by {:kind :agent :id "..."}
 :basis [source-extract-id ...]
 :confidence {:value 0.62
              :method :human-provisional
              :model-version nil}
 :status :provisional}
```

The future graph becomes a projection of these records, not the source that invents their authority.

## The process charter

I would make the new `PROCESS.md` a charter with **a few hard invariants and a delegation model**, not an exhaustive rulebook.

Possible opening:

```md
# Epiphany Process Charter

## Purpose

Epiphany's process exists to help people and agents build understanding,
make bounded commitments, preserve evidence, and revise course without
misrepresenting uncertainty or acceptance.

## Constitutional invariant

No actor may silently promote an observation into an inference, an inference
into a decision, a plan into completion, or a similarity claim into identity.

## Operating commitments

- Work begins with an explicit current understanding of the requested outcome.
- Material commitments name their target, scope, basis, and stop conditions.
- Evidence is proportional to consequence; policies determine the required form.
- Completion claims are supported by recorded observed verification evidence.
- A decision is accepted only through its declared authority.
- Exceptions are explicit, scoped, time-bound, and reviewable.
- Each completed phase leaves a successor enough context to continue, challenge,
  or revise the work.
```

That captures the hard thing you were reaching for without locking you into a fixed test regime or an elaborate actor protocol.

A few definitions are particularly important for the charter rewrite:

- A **document** is human-readable communication; a **record** preserves a discrete structured fact/event/result.

- A **finding** is derived; it is not an accepted decision.
- A **passing test** is an observed verification result; it does not prove all intended behavior.
- **Acceptance** requires a scoped authority and a stated basis.
- An **exception** is temporary and reviewable; it cannot silently become policy.

## Preserve the kernel

The style guide should be **adjusted, renamed in place if desired, and made explicit as cross-project governance**. It should not be treated as a temporary project policy like Kanban or research practice.

Its core invariant is sound:

```text
Category: what kind of transformation/event can exist?
Contract: may this particular instance count as valid now?
```

That is more useful than a generic “clean architecture” prescription because it separates two sources of confusion:

- A domain model may describe a lawful state transition without deciding whether current policy allows it.
- A schema/contract may reject a malformed value without deciding domain meaning.
- A foreign Java object may be technically valid to a library but still be invalid at the Epiphany boundary.
- An infrastructure adapter may orchestrate effects without becoming the place where policy or semantics are improvised.


## The five semantic layers

I would retain all five names and make their roles sharper:


| Layer | Core responsibility | May depend on | Must not do |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| `law.*` | Schemas, predicates, contract declarations, error shapes, boundary admissibility | Pure Clojure libraries only | I/O, orchestration, domain policy |
| `shape.*` | Pure transformations between declared shapes: parsing, normalization, encoding, decoding | `law.*` | I/O, hidden coercion, domain decisions |
| `extern.*` | Raw JVM/Java/SDK/filesystem/network/database boundary interaction; decode foreign values immediately | Java SDKs, libraries, `shape.*`, `law.*` | Leak foreign objects upward or make domain policy |
| `domain.*` | Pure Epiphany decisions and state-transition logic over trusted shapes | `law.*`, `shape.*` | I/O, SDK imports, raw external types |
| `infra.*` | Effect orchestration and adapter composition | All lower layers | Conceal domain policy or directly invent unvalidated records |

That has a concrete implication for what we saw today: a Java library call does **not** belong directly in `domain.*`, and an `infra.*` adapter should not hand a raw JGit/Mongo/Lucene/Ollama response to domain logic. The `extern.*` boundary converts it into a declared `law.*` shape or returns an explicit boundary failure.

## Schemas are not optional

Your instinct about missing schemas is dead on. In Epiphany, law is not just validation hygiene; it is how the code keeps epistemic categories from collapsing.

Examples of things that deserve `law.*` schemas/contracts:

- Observation records: source identity, observation method, timestamp/context, result state.
- Status/tier values: observed, derived, provisional, accepted, rejected, stale, unavailable.
- Continuity candidates: source/target, relation type, basis, confidence/method, reviewer decision.
- Repository identity records and Git-local metadata.
- Command inputs and application-port contracts.
- Persistence operation registry entries and stored-record versions.
- Boundary error envelopes, especially distinction between unknown, unavailable, corrupt, invalid, and not implemented.
- Markdown extraction records: revision, path bytes/string representation policy, heading/span context, extractor version.
- Evidence packet manifests and research findings.

A well-tested domain function cannot compensate for accepting unshaped foreign data or persisting malformed records. That is why missing schemas were an early warning signal before feature-by-feature interrogation.

## Categories versus contracts

I would preserve the existing language but add an operational test:

> **A category tells the reader what sort of thing this is. A contract tells the system whether this concrete thing may cross this boundary or count as this category now.**

For example:

```clojure
;; Category/domain concept:
{:continuity/kind :same-path-adjacent-commit}

;; Contract/law:
{:continuity/source-expression-id ...
 :continuity/target-expression-id ...
 :continuity/kind :same-path-adjacent-commit
 :continuity/basis {:commit-a ... :commit-b ... :path-bytes ...}
 :epistemic-tier :observed}
```

The category is not enough to permit persistence, presentation, or acceptance. The law defines the evidence and fields required for that instance to count as an observed continuity record.

Likewise, an `:accepted` tier is category-valid only if the contract requires a review/acceptance event and authority reference. That makes “human acceptance is explicit” executable rather than aspirational.

## Construction order remains good

Keep the construction order, but adapt its ClojureScript-specific wording:

```text
Discovery
  -> describe
  -> specify
  -> define law
  -> shape
  -> extern
  -> domain
  -> infra
  -> verify
  -> reflect
```

The sequence does not mean every task must produce seven namespaces. It means a layer may only be skipped when it is genuinely irrelevant, not because an agent wants to reach a working demo quickly.

For a pure domain change, `extern.*` may be absent. For a new Java library integration, it is not optional. For a new persisted record, `law.*` should precede adapter code. For an external response, the adapter’s first job is to decode/decontaminate it at the boundary.

Before we write document governance, we should revise `STYLE.md` into an **Epiphany-compatible Engineering Kernel** and then derive a change plan from it.

The rewrite should:

Yes. This is a better expression of the kernel because it makes the construction order a **compositional program**, not a linear checklist:

$$
(\eta\ \text{Discovery} \rightarrow ((\text{Describe} \rightarrow \text{specify} \rightarrow \text{define}) \rightarrow (\mu\ \text{shape} \rightarrow \text{extern} \rightarrow \text{domain} \rightarrow \text{infra})) \rightarrow \Pi)
$$

It also preserves the distinction that matters most: **η is encounter before explanation; μ is capability before explanation; Π is the integrated product whose behavior is continuously checked against both.** A mathematical vector field is conventionally understood as assigning a vector at each point of a space/manifold, so the metaphor works if the “space” is the current problem/context and the vector is a directional effect or capability available there.[^25_1][^25_2]

## Reading the expression

I would give the expression an operational reading rather than pretending it is formal category theory:

```text
η Discovery
  -> ((Describe -> specify -> define)
      -> (μ shape -> extern -> domain -> infra))
  -> Π
```

| Symbol/group | Process meaning | Concrete artifact/evidence |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| `η` | Experienced effects whose source is not yet understood | Anomalies, user reports, failing tests, observed behavior, foreign responses, existing code |
| `Discovery` | Orient to η: inspect, inventory, and preserve observations without premature explanation | Discovery note, source references, command output, anomaly log |
| `(Describe -> specify -> define)` | Convert uncertain experience into explicit intended semantics and admissibility contracts | Intent, acceptance criteria, `law.*` schemas, predicates, error shapes |
| `μ` | Capabilities we can produce but whose full explanation may not yet be complete | Candidate transformations, experiments, parsers, adapters, prototypes |
| `(shape -> extern -> domain -> infra)` | Refine μ into bounded implementation: pure shapes, foreign boundary, pure decisions, effect composition | Namespaces, tests, decoded values, adapters, operational behavior |
| `Π` | Integrated product: the result made inspectable through continuous integration and verification | Green CI run, test/contract results, review evidence, releaseable integration |

## η: experienced effects

Your η definition gives the anomaly rule a deeper basis.

η is not “a bug ticket” or “requirements.” It is the field of effects encountered before we have an adequate causal model:

- A user says the output is wrong.
- A Git history rewrite appears to break apparent continuity.
- JGit returns an object shape that does not fit the expected mental model.
- A test fails.
- A library API has behavior we can produce or observe but cannot yet explain.
- Existing code has a shape that contradicts the planned shape.

η therefore begins with **observation**, not interpretation. An agent can collect and name an η-effect, but it must not claim to know its source merely because it has a plausible explanation.

That maps directly to the Charter’s epistemic rule: observed effect is not derived cause, derived cause is not accepted explanation, and similarity is not identity.

## Describe, specify, define

This first parenthesized group is the semantic/conceptual compression step:

```text
Describe: What effect/problem are we trying to account for?

Specify: What must count as success, failure, or an admissible result?

Define: What laws/contracts make those judgments executable?
```

The critical point is that `define` creates `law.*`, not an implementation plan.

For Epiphany, this is where schemas live:

```clojure
;; law/observation.clj, illustrative only
{:observation/id ...
 :observation/source ...
 :observation/method ...
 :observation/recorded-at ...
 :observation/status :observed}
```

A record becomes structurally admissible as an observation here. It does **not** become a domain conclusion, a persistence implementation, or an accepted interpretation.

## μ: producible effects

μ is a useful counterweight to η.

η says: *something affects us, but we do not yet know why.*

μ says: *we can make something happen, but we do not yet have a sufficient explanation of its properties, limits, or consequences.*

Examples:

- An agent can invoke a Java API that appears to return commits.
- A parser can extract headings.
- A Mongo write can persist a document.
- An embedding model can generate a similarity score.
- A prototype can make a UI state appear.

None is yet adequate system behavior. μ capability becomes trustworthy only as it is shaped, bounded, modeled, and integrated.

This is why direct Java/SDK calls create architectural risk: raw foreign capability lands too close to domain logic, letting μ masquerade as a fully understood internal concept.

## Shape through infrastructure

The second group turns raw capability into a system behavior with accountable boundaries:

```text
μ
  -> shape
  -> extern
  -> domain
  -> infra
```


### `shape.*`

Pure transformations make data legible and composable: parse, encode, decode, normalize only where policy permits, and transform values between declared shapes.

A shape function should be able to say:

> Given an admissible input shape, here is the output shape; no I/O, hidden state, or domain policy was smuggled in.

### `extern.*`

`extern.*` is where the project meets Java/JGit, Mongo, Lucene, Ollama, filesystem, HTTP, operating-system processes, or browser/Node APIs.

It owns two duties:

1. Invoke the foreign capability.
2. Convert foreign values and failures into declared internal shapes or explicit boundary errors **immediately**.

Nothing above it should need to reason directly about raw Java classes, `Optional`, driver cursors, SDK exception hierarchies, HTTP-client response objects, or library-specific nullability.

### `domain.*`

`domain.*` makes pure Epiphany decisions over internal, shaped values:

- Is this relation observed, derived, provisional, or accepted?
- Does this candidate satisfy the evidence requirements to be shown?
- Is a repository registration idempotent?
- Is a path-preserving continuity claim valid under the chosen policy?
- Must a result be `UNAVAILABLE` rather than empty?

Domain code can use the laws but does not perform foreign effects.

### `infra.*`

`infra.*` composes actual effects: it wires ports, selects adapters, orchestrates operations, and translates domain decisions into boundary calls. It must not become a dumping ground where persistence/API details and unexamined business policy fuse together.

## Π: product through integration

Π should not mean merely “the build is green.” It is the **currently integrated, inspectable product** produced by the cycle.

Continuous integration is the mechanism that repeatedly asks whether the pieces still compose. The term generally refers to integrating changes frequently and validating the integrated state through automated build/test feedback; your process can broaden that idea to include contract checks, architecture checks, documentation checks, and review evidence.[^25_2]

For Epiphany, Π has at least four components:

```text
Π =
  integrated code
  + declared contracts
  + verification records
  + reviewable documentation/evidence
```

A green test suite is evidence *about* Π, not Π itself. A merged branch is a repository event, not automatic acceptance of the product outcome.

## One needed clarification

I would avoid calling η and μ literally “vector fields” in the style guide unless you intend to define the space and composition enough to make that mathematically meaningful. In mathematics, a vector field assigns a tangent vector to every point of a manifold; your usage is more accurately an intentional **process metaphor** for directional fields of encountered and producible effects.[^25_1][^25_2]

That does not weaken the idea. It keeps the charter honest:

```md
η and μ are process metaphors, not a claim that the development workflow is a
formal differential-geometric model. They name two kinds of partial knowledge:
experienced effects without adequate causal account, and producible effects
without adequate explanatory account.
```
